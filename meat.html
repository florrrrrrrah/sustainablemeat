Could Future Meat Alternatives Help Us Achieve Sustainability?

Chiayu Wang, last updated on JULY 15 2024

As the global population becomes increasingly aware of the environmental and health impacts of meat consumption, the demand for sustainable solutions appears to be declining in the UK, particularly following the COVID-19 pandemic. What stops consumers from taking in meat alternatives? Are there any other future choices could help us achieve sustainability?

Way Back into Meat as Flexitarians Decrease

A growing number of people in the UK are reducing their meat consumption for health and environmental causes. A survey by the Smart Protein project on dietary lifestyles shows that 48% of British people cut down on meat intake in 2023, an increase of 11% compared to 2021.

Instead of going vegetarian or vegan directly, some people are adopting a flexitarian diet on a trial basis. Flexitarians actively incorporate plant-based meals into their diet while also embracing some meat and other animal foods in moderation, a practice also known as casual vegetarianism.

However, the latest statistics suggest a slightly reverse trend in the UK. Britons appear to be falling back in love with meat again and leave their flexitarian diet behind.

The proportion of flexitarians reached its highest at 16% in 2022 but shortly reverted to the previous levels. In contrast, the figure of meat eaters has been steadily increasing since 2021, while the percentages of vegan, vegetarian, and pescetarian diets have remained relatively low over the last five years.

Protein Competition Intensifies BUT Market Facing Downturns

This flexible dietary change previously gave rise to the rapid development of meat alternatives, which offer supplemental nutrients for vegan and vegetarian meals.

According to the Good Food Institute, the UK ranks Europe’s second biggest market of plant-based meat and had the third highest average consumption in 2022. The Meat alternative sector is also the most developed among all categories in the UK plant-based food market, reaching £474.5 million in revenue during the same period.

Even so, recent research by Mintel reveals a continuous decline in the value and volume of meat alternatives since 2022, after the market enjoyed strong growth amid the COVID-19 lockdowns.

Retail sales of meat alternatives in the UK dropped 4% in 2023, following a failing 2022 with revenue declines of 6.8%. In terms of quantity, meat alternatives sold remained to decrease in both years, facing downturns of over 12% consecutively. The recession is expected to persist until 2026, in which the meat alternatives market may regain its momentum.

Quorn, the UK’s leading brand in meat alternatives, encountered similar stagnation, reporting a 4% revenue decline earlier this year attributed to “continued category softness”.

A Quorn spokesperson explains: “The market has been inundated with new product and brand launches in recent years, and these have not always kept shoppers coming back.” Quorn aims to address consumers’ major concerns—affordability and taste—when it comes to purchasing meat alternatives.

The declining sales and consumption is predominantly a result of inflation and rising prices post-pandemic, despite the ongoing trend of eating sustainably for future generations. According to Statista, 2021 marked a turning point for the meat alternative market. It saw the fastest growth by 23.5% in consumption and the average price dropped to its lowest.

A stabilising trajectory emerged following the market’s success in 2021, with consumption reaching a higher level than in 2019 and the price staying at around £9. Price plays a crucial role in this market, significantly influencing consumption patterns. While the initial adoption of plant-based meat was strong, sustained high prices appear to have led to a plateau in future consumption.

The rise of meat alternatives presents a long-term threat to the traditional meat market. ​​In 2022, plant-based meat prices increased by 4% on average, whereas pre-packaged conventional meat prices rose by 10%.

The latest report from the Agriculture and Horticulture Development Board (AHDB) demonstrates that the average price of a main meal with meat alternatives is nearly double that of a meat-free meal, aligning closely with the costs of a meal containing traditional meat. Statistics also indicate that plant-based meat alternative products were 3.3% more expensive than their animal-based counterparts.

Once the prices of meat alternatives drop and financial pressure eases, the appeal of sustainability is expected to drive 28% of processed meat buyers to consider consuming more plant-based meat products or protein-rich ingredients, according to a Mintel’s survey.

In response to the growing trend towards green dietary choices, several global chain restaurants, including TGI Fridays and Taco Bell, have begun to embrace plant-based meat into their menu offerings.

Temple of Seitan, one of London’s earliest established vegan fast food restaurants, further partnered with famous tofu brand Tofoo to launch chicken flavoured faux meat products in May.

Anbessa Archibald has been working in the Temple of Seitan’s kitchen for over a year. He has not only observed an increase in vegan diners at the restaurant, but also noticed many tourists and omnivores giving seitan a try for the first time.

As a vegan himself, Anbessa favours wholefoods in his diet and often prepares his own meat alternatives, tempeh, rather than purchasing them from the supermarkets due to limited choices. He explains: “One of the advantages of the seitan we offer in our restaurant is its high protein content, which provides essential nutrients for vegans, vegetarians, and even bodybuilders.”

To A More Sustainable Dining Table

Instead of solely eyeing on the traditional meat competitors and innovating their alternatives for vegan or vegetarian meals, Marco Bertacca, CEO of Quorn, is rethinking the future of their products following the downfall.

“We now find ourselves collaborating to offer less-meat options to consumers who are looking to reduce meat consumption, but not eat vegetarian or vegan meals.” Bertacca says, noting this approach caters to the majority and presents a significant opportunity to reduce carbon emissions in the food system and improve public health.

Another popular sustainable choice is claimed to be cultivated meat, also known as lab-grown meat, aims to effectuate meat production without having an animal slaughtered and emitting substantial carbon emissions.

Ivy Farm Technologies, one of the earliest cultivated meat companies and sponsored by Oxford University, has successfully produced chicken, pork, beef, and even duck meat in laboratories.

Riley Jackson, Partnerships and Marketing Manager at Ivy Farm Technologies describes the process: “Producing cultivated meat is similar to taking a cutting from a plant and providing it with all the nutrients and the environment to grow.” She emphasises the highly monitored and regulated nature of the process, dramatically reducing risks of contamination compared to traditional farming methods.

Before joining Ivy Farm Technologies, Jackson also worked at a plant-based meat company. She acknowledges that “plant-based meat has come a really long way, but has not yet lived up to the promises of tasting as good or better than meat from a cow or a pig”.

Protein alternative has also become one of the UK government’s resort to achieve Net Zero goals. However, the controversy surrounding cultivated meat remains unsettled, with only three countries—Singapore, the US, and Israel—having approved it for human consumption.

Ivy Farm Technologies, along with other cultivated meat companies, is collaborating closely with the UK government to secure approval from the Food Standards Agency (FSA). Jackson anticipates that cultivated meat will be officially approved in the UK next year, but acknowledges that it will take several more years for their products to be mass-produced and available on the market.

While plant-based and cultivated meats offer promising solutions for reducing environmental impact, their success depends on overcoming current challenges such as cost and taste. To achieve sustainability by shifting dietary habits and reducing meat intake, safe meat alternative choices are proven to be what consumers need for cutting down carbon emission.
